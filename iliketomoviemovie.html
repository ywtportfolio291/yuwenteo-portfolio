I Like to Movie Movie! — Cinema Scheduling Simulation Game

I led the product design process for a multi-month project to develop an interactive cinema scheduling game. Our mission was to challenge players by simulating the intricacies of real-world cinema operations—helping them hone their strategic decision-making skills and adaptability to changing audience preferences.

The Challenge

Movie exhibitors face a complex puzzle: deciding which films to screen, how often, and at what times. This is known as the Movie Scheduling Problem (MSP)—a task made more complicated by:

  • Varying Demand — weekends vs. weekdays require different strategies
  • Film Runtimes & Cleaning Intervals — precise timing is needed to avoid overlaps
  • Rental Costs & Profit Margins — balancing cost vs. audience draw
  • Hall Capacity Constraints — every seat counts toward profitability

To make these challenges interactive and teachable, we distilled the Movie Scheduling Problem into five core constraints—each directly modeled in the game:

  • Hall Capacity — each hall has a fixed number of seats
  • Dynamic Demand — audience interest shifts by time of day and movie freshness
  • Runtimes & Cleaning Buffers — schedules must account for turnover time
  • Rental Costs vs. Revenue — films incur fixed costs that cut into profits
  • Overage / Underage Risk — over- or under-scheduling leads to missed revenue

Instead of relying on complex optimization algorithms, we designed a game that visualizes the real-world trade-offs of scheduling. Players learn through intuitive decision-making and live feedback—not formulas—by testing strategies in a risk-free, interactive simulation.

Design Discovery

With the Movie Scheduling Problem broken down into core mechanics, the next step was understanding who we were designing for — and what kind of game experience would best support learning and decision-making.

We conducted interviews with a diverse group of stakeholders across the cinema ecosystem, including:

  • Cinema managers
  • Cleaning and operations staff
  • Indie filmmakers
  • Business stakeholders (e.g., brand sponsors and executives)

These conversations helped us shape four representative personas — each capturing a unique mix of influence, interest, and learning motivation.

Power-influence matrix

What Users Wanted

  • High-interest learners wanted a hands-on simulation to teach scheduling without technical knowledge.
  • Operational staff valued realism — especially around cleaning and turnover logistics.
  • Newcomers needed gentle onboarding and clear in-game guidance.
  • Decision-makers preferred short, engaging sessions with meaningful takeaways.

Survey & Market Insights

To validate our design direction, we conducted surveys and benchmarked existing simulation games. From this, we identified three pillars of effective learning-through-play:

  • Intuitive — Clear, easy-to-navigate interface
  • Educational — Feedback systems that help players reflect and improve
  • Entertaining — Gameplay that engages without sacrificing strategy

How It Shaped the Game

These insights directly informed the gameplay experience:

  • Drag-and-drop interface to simplify scheduling interactions
  • Real-time visual summaries to help players learn from decisions
  • Adjustable difficulty levels for different user types
  • UI patterns modeled after real-world cinema workflows

Game Ideation

Initial Prototype

Our first build featured a drag-and-drop scheduling prototype. Players were tasked with assigning films to available time slots across cinema halls, with a movie list anchored at the bottom of the screen — a familiar layout inspired by casual games. At the end of the game, there will be a customer feedback module and a summarized performance of the player's revenue and demand of each movie.

However, through internal testing and feedback from our professor, two key usability issues emerged:

  • The bottom-anchored movie list caused excessive cursor movement and made it harder to cross-reference hall availability and time slots.
  • The customer feedback module, while visually engaging, lacked strategic value and felt redundant.

Pivot: From Playful to Purposeful

Based on this feedback, we made two core refinements:

  • Sidebar Redesign: Shifted the movie selection panel to the left side, streamlining user interactions and improving visibility.

  • Strategic Dashboard: Replaced the generic customer feedback module with a robust statistics dashboard. Players could now review real demand, profit margins, rental costs, and day-over-day trends—encouraging reflection and informed planning.

Gameplay & Simulation Logic

The core gameplay loop mirrors the real-world challenges of cinema scheduling. It incorporates operational constraints, financial trade-offs, and a structured 14-day simulation cycle.

This section provides a structured overview of how the game functions — from login to leaderboard — and explains how each system supports strategic thinking and learning through play.

1. Login & Onboarding Flow

Walkthrough of the login and onboarding flow — from user registration to gameplay entry.

Players begin by registering or logging into the game. The system offers two paths:

  • Register: Generate a username and create a password
  • Login: Enter previously saved credentials

After a successful login, players can choose to either:

  • Run a short tutorial to understand game mechanics
  • Jump straight into gameplay

This step ensures accessibility for first-time users while allowing experienced players to skip ahead.

2. Initial Conditions Setup

Once the player clicks “Start Game,” the system initializes the conditions needed for Day 1 of the simulation. The setup mimics how a real cinema operator would start their scheduling day — with a clear view of resources and constraints.

First look at the main scheduling interface — featuring the drag-and-drop movie grid and live feedback panel.

  • Cash Balance: Every player starts with a fixed amount — $10,000 — which they’ll use to pay movie rental costs.
  • Day Counter: Set to Day 1 of a 14-day simulation.
  • Movie List: Pulled from the database, including genre, runtime, popularity, and how many days the movie has been in circulation.
  • Scheduling Grid: A blank planner that mirrors a cinema layout — 4 halls, each broken down into 10-minute slots across the operating day.

On the frontend, players see their username and cash in the header, a list of movies on the left, and the blank schedule in the center — ready for drag-and-drop planning.

3. 14-Day Simulation Loop

The core gameplay unfolds across a 14-day in-game cycle. Players drag and drop films onto the scheduling grid, while the system injects realistic cinema constraints like ad buffers and cleaning times.

Drag-and-Drop functionality across all 4 cinema halls

  • Drag-and-Drop Scheduling: Players build each day’s lineup by placing films into grid slots.
  • Conflict Detection: The system checks for overlapping times and prevents scheduling errors.
  • Operational Buffers: Ads and cleaning intervals are automatically slotted in to simulate real cinema workflows.
  • End-of-Day Summary: Reports show ad revenue, ticket revenue, rental costs, and net profits — giving players direct feedback.

4. Embedded Strategic Mechanics

As players progress, dynamic systems introduce challenges and deepen strategy:

Different scenarioes - weekends/holidays that lead to different ticket prices and surge in movie demand

  • Variable Rental Costs: Genre, runtime, popularity influence cost
  • Dynamic Ticket Pricing: Weekends/holidays offer higher revenue
  • Limited Film Availability: Films expire over time, adding urgency

5. Strategic Pacing & Game Completion

We structured the simulation as a 14-day cycle to avoid fatigue while balancing engagement and learning. This timeframe allows players to:

  • Test different scheduling strategies across weekdays, weekends, and holidays
  • Adapt to shifting movie availability and performance feedback in real time
  • Reflect and improve using hall utilization and movie-specific data visualizations

By integrating intuitive interactions, financial logic, and adaptive mechanics, our game delivers an experience that is both educational and engaging. Each subsystem — from login to the statistical dashboard — reinforces strategic thinking in cinema scheduling.

Challenges

White borders appearing

One of the key challenges I tackled was maintaining layout stability as our drag-and-drop interface evolved. Frequent UI changes introduced styling issues like misalignment and element overlap.

I addressed this by restructuring our CSS using modular utility classes, a grid-based layout system, and scalable spacing variables. This not only improved maintainability but also ensured consistent behavior across different screen sizes and browsers.

The result was a cleaner, more responsive interface that enhanced usability — especially during intensive interactions like film scheduling across multiple halls.

Learnings

Early in the design process, I explored a range of game ideas related to cinema operations, including:

Cinema Queue Rush: A fast-paced ticketing game where players manage queues and seating in real time. This prototype tested mechanics around time pressure, customer flow, and bottlenecks.

Cinema Deal: A card-based strategy game centered on acquiring film IPs and negotiating revenue shares. It explored profitability models and long-term planning under uncertainty.

Cinema Tycoon (Board Game): A spin-based multiplayer game that challenged players to collect cinema halls, buy upgrades, and sabotage opponents. While it was engaging, the mechanics didn’t directly reinforce movie scheduling trade-offs.

Although these ideas offered interesting gameplay, they didn’t fully address the Movie Scheduling Problem (MSP)—our core challenge. Through reflection and feedback, I pivoted toward a more focused simulation experience.

This design pivot taught me to:

  • Frame a clear problem early — to guide design decisions and avoid feature creep
  • Recognize when to diverge vs. converge — balancing creative exploration with delivery focus
  • Let go of fun ideas that don’t serve the mission — even if they’re well-developed

Narrowing the scope ultimately helped me design a more cohesive, educational, and strategically aligned experience. One that teaches players how to make smarter scheduling decisions in a simulated cinema setting.
